Buffer is a tool that allows people to schedule their social media posts in advance. In this analysis we will explore how far in advance users schedule their posts.

To do this we will compute some summary statistics for users that have scheduled posts in the past month. Then, we will summarise those summary statistics to try to understand the overall population of users.

Data Collection

We’ll gather one million updates that were created in the past month.

select 
  up.id as update_id
  , up.user_id as user_id
  , u.billing_plan_name as plan
  , up.created_at
  , up.sent_at
  , datediff(hour, up.created_at, up.sent_at) as hours_to_send
  , up.profile_service
from dbt.updates as up
inner join dbt.users as u
  on up.user_id = u.id
where up.created_at > current_date - 180
and up.was_sent_with_buffer
and up.created_at < current_date - 60
order by random()
limit 1000000

We have around 900 thousand updates created in the past six months. Let’s plot the distribution of the number of hours in advance people scheduled their posts.

As we can see, most posts are scheduled to go out in a very short period of time. There is a long tail of updates that were scheduled far in advance. Now let’s calculate the 75th percentile for all users. This will tell us, for each user, the number of hours for which 75% of their posts were scheduled in advance.

We’ll only include users that scheduled at least 4 posts

# calculate 75% quantile
by_user <- updates %>% 
  group_by(user_id) %>% 
  summarise(updates = n_distinct(update_id),
            percentile = quantile(hours_to_send, probs = 0.75)[[1]]) %>% 
  filter(updates >= 4)
